# The History of Angkor: A Student's Guide

The Angkorian period (802-1431 CE) represents one of history's most remarkable civilizations. For students visiting Cambodia, understanding this era transforms temple visits from architectural appreciation into profound historical encounters.

## Origins: The Foundation of Angkor (802 CE)

The Khmer Empire began when King Jayavarman II unified competing kingdoms and declared himself chakravartin (universal ruler) on Mount Kulen. This declaration established the concept of divine kingship that would define Angkorian politics for centuries.

## The Early Period: Indravarman I (877-889 CE)

Indravarman I established the empire's economic foundation through sophisticated water management. He built the first large baray (reservoir) and established the precedent of temple-mountain architecture, linking royal authority to both divine and earthly prosperity.

## The Golden Age: Rajendravarman II (944-968 CE)

This period saw the construction of Pre Rup and East Mebon, featuring sophisticated engineering and artistic achievements. The empire expanded its influence throughout Southeast Asia through both military conquest and cultural diplomacy.

## The Apex: Suryavarman II (1113-1150 CE)

Suryavarman II built Angkor Wat, dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu. This architectural masterpiece represented the peak of Khmer artistry and engineering, requiring enormous resources and labor. Its bas-reliefs depict both mythological scenes and historical court life.

## The Buddhist Transformation: Jayavarman VII (1181-1218 CE)

After a devastating Cham invasion, Jayavarman VII rebuilt the empire and converted to Mahayana Buddhism. He built Angkor Thom, Bayon, and Ta Prohm, shifting from divine kingship to compassionate Buddhist rule. His reign saw unprecedented construction and the establishment of a network of hospitals throughout the empire.

## The Decline and Fall

The 14th and 15th centuries brought multiple challenges: repeated Thai invasions, religious tension between Hindus and Buddhists, and ecological stress from deforestation and water management failures. In 1431, the Khmer court abandoned Angkor for Phnom Penh, though the reasons remain debated among historians.

## Why Angkor Matters

Angkor represents Southeast Asia's greatest pre-colonial civilization. Its achievements in architecture, engineering, urban planning, and art rival those of contemporary civilizations worldwide. Understanding Angkor is essential to understanding Southeast Asian history.

## Visiting with Historical Context

When exploring the temples, consider: Who built this and why? What did it represent in its time? How has its meaning changed? What does it reveal about the people who created it?

Asking these questions transforms sightseeing into historical inquiry, making your visit an act of discovery rather than consumption.
